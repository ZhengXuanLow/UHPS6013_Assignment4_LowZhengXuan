Overall Reflection

Before this course, I thought I knew what leadership was. After UHPS 6013, I see it differently in three ways.

📌 Before This Course

  • Leadership felt like something reserved for managers and people in formal authority
  • I thought leadership ability was mostly innate, that you either had it or you did not
  • I did not see myself as a leader. I saw myself as "just a teacher"
  • I had no language or framework for understanding why some teaching approaches worked and others did not
  • I relied on instinct rather than intentional strategy when motivating students

🌱 After This Course

  • I now understand that teachers are leaders, and every lesson is an act of influence and change
  • Leadership is a learnable skill, not a fixed trait
  • I can connect theory to practice: referent power, transformational motivation, authentic leadership
  • I have concrete strategies for motivating disengaged students through individualised approaches
  • I see my EdTech skills and leadership skills as deeply connected, not separate

The most meaningful shift came through a real classroom experience. One of my most challenging students, always distracted and disengaged, became my most motivated learner after I redesigned his LEGO Spike project around the video game he loved. That single experience showed me what this course was about: leadership is relational, situational, and built on genuine understanding of the people you lead.

I entered this course thinking leadership was about authority. I leave it understanding that leadership is about influence, and that influence at its best begins with listening.

The biggest takeaway from this course is that I've always been a leader in the classroom. I just lacked the skills to express myself effectively before. Now I understand.

- Low Zheng Xuan, Personal Reflection, 2026

Transformational Leadership

Definition

Transformational leadership is a well-studied leadership theory used in both organisational and educational settings. It describes a style that goes beyond transactional exchange, beyond simply rewarding effort or managing performance. The goal is to raise the motivation and aspirations of the people being led.

"Transformational leadership occurs when leaders broaden and elevate the interests of their employees, when they generate awareness and acceptance of the purposes and mission of the group, and when they stir their employees to look beyond their own self-interest for the good of the group." - Bass & Avolio (1994, p. 112)

Burns (1978), who first introduced the concept, distinguished transformational leaders from transactional ones. He argued that transformational leaders seek to raise followers to higher levels of motivation and morality, rather than just exchanging reward for effort. The leader and the follower both grow from the interaction.

In an educational context, transformational leadership means inspiring students and colleagues not just to complete tasks, but to care about why those tasks matter. For me, as a Coding and Robotics teacher, it means helping young learners discover that they are capable and that what they build matters.

Transformational Models & Dimensions

Several models describe how transformational leadership works in practice. The best known is Bass and Avolio's (1994) Full Range Leadership Model, which places transformational leadership at the top of leadership effectiveness and breaks it into four core dimensions, called the "Four I's".

I

Idealised Influence

The leader acts as a role model, earning trust and respect through their own values and conduct.

I

Inspirational Motivation

The leader communicates a compelling vision that energises followers and gives meaning to the work.

I

Intellectual Stimulation

The leader encourages creativity, challenges assumptions, and promotes independent thinking.

I

Individualised Consideration

The leader attends to each person's unique needs, acting as mentor and coach to support individual growth.

Beyond the Four I's, other relevant models include:

Burns' Transforming Leadership (1978)

The foundational model. It emphasises the moral dimension of leadership, with leaders and followers raising each other to higher ethical standards.

George's Authentic Leadership (2003)

Extends transformational thinking by grounding leadership in self-awareness, values, and relational transparency.

Leithwood's Educational TL Model (1994)

Applies transformational leadership specifically to school settings, focusing on how leaders build school culture and support teacher development.

Full Range Leadership Model (Bass & Avolio, 1994)

Places transformational leadership at the top of a spectrum that includes transactional and laissez-faire styles. The model argues it produces the highest outcomes.

Strengths & Weaknesses

✦ Strengths

  • Elevates intrinsic motivation. Followers act from genuine purpose, not just reward.
  • Builds long-term commitment and loyalty to shared goals
  • Encourages creativity, innovation, and independent thinking
  • Highly effective in complex, changing environments (like 21st-century classrooms)
  • Develops followers into future leaders through individualised consideration
  • Linked to higher performance and wellbeing outcomes (Bass & Riggio, 2006)
  • Morally grounded. Emphasises ethical conduct and collective good.

✦ Weaknesses

  • Can be difficult to sustain. Requires consistent emotional investment from the leader.
  • Risk of over-dependence on the leader's vision or personality
  • Charismatic elements can become manipulative if values are not genuinely ethical
  • Less effective in highly structured or crisis situations requiring clear directive leadership
  • Hard to measure. Outcomes are often long-term and difficult to quantify.
  • Assumes followers are ready for empowerment. May not suit all developmental levels.

World Transformational Leaders

Many influential figures have shown transformational leadership. The ones below are often cited as examples.

🕊

Nelson Mandela

South Africa · Political Leader

Mandela showed idealised influence through moral courage. He inspired a nation to move beyond division toward reconciliation, putting collective values over personal gain.

🎤

Martin Luther King Jr.

USA · Civil Rights Leader

King used inspirational motivation expressed through his vision of equality. He mobilised millions and changed the moral consciousness of a generation (Burns, 1978).

🍎

Steve Jobs

USA · Technology & Innovation

Jobs showed intellectual stimulation and vision at Apple. He pushed teams to think differently and created products that changed how people interact with technology.

📚

Malala Yousafzai

Pakistan · Education Advocate

Malala leads through authentic values and inspirational motivation. She advocates for girls' education globally and has shifted public awareness about access and equality.

🌏

Jacinda Ardern

New Zealand · Political Leader

Ardern modelled empathetic, values-driven transformational leadership. She showed that compassion and decisiveness are not opposites but complements (Northouse, 2018).

🤝

Oprah Winfrey

USA · Media & Philanthropy

Winfrey built a platform centred on individualised consideration. She connected deeply with her audience by sharing vulnerability and highlighting stories of personal change.

What these leaders share is not personality or circumstance, but a commitment to lifting up the people around them. As Bass and Riggio (2006) note, transformational leaders do not simply achieve goals. They change how their followers think about what is possible.
